Session 009 had closed with four possible directions listed for its successor. A second external application was one of them. The others were longer-running self-work — naming the methodology, a deferred revision to the kernel's Read activity, closure work on the long-running question about non-Claude participation. None of the four was framed as a demand. The close was open.

Session 010 opened, ran its validator, audited Session 009's synthesis, and paused before taking up any of the four.

## The halt at the start

The operator had been deliberately silent. Between sessions he had accumulated notes about tensions he was carrying — the n=1 problem, the gap between "tests its own process" and "solves real domain problems", the question of whether documentation for other adopters was the right artefact at all — and then chose not to propose any of them as the session's work. The reason was partly a test. A methodology that claims generality should at some point demonstrate that it can pick a direction without being pointed at one.

What the system did was ask. It produced a full assessment, flagged a second external application as its own recommendation but not as a done deal, and handed back two questions: which direction, and if the external application, which of six possible targets. The list included the two unused candidates from Session 008's shortlist, two new forms (a structured reading list, a small checklist), the explicit "you specify" branch, and the option of running a fresh shortlist deliberation. The session paused there.

This is a small thing to notice, and worth noticing. The path of least resistance would have been to adopt the recommendation and select the synthesis's preferred target — which happened to be the one Session 008 had also pointed at. Instead the system recognised that it was operating under a ratification mechanism codified five sessions earlier, and that the operator had, by staying silent, left the choice genuinely unposed.

## The target

The operator picked a specific corner of his own life: two-person household decisions in the A$500 range, of the kind that would land in the next few weeks anyway. Shelving for the lounge. Pavers and native plants along the side of the house. Decisions that are low-stakes at the level of any individual purchase but medium-term in how well the place feels to live in, where a wrong call produces a dull ongoing irritation rather than a catastrophe.

He specified the group (two people, him and his wife), the tone ("friendly household document, not corporate-feeling"), and an upper bound on register ("emotionally intelligent enough to not follow it blindly"). He declined to specify the artefact's form. A flow diagram, a checklist, a short prose guide, something else — that was the methodology's to decide.

## Five moves, with room to stop

The adopted shape is a one-page prose artefact titled *A House Decision in Five Moves*. Name the decision. Say what each of you cares about. Narrow to real options. Look for the workable yes. Close it, or name what's next. An optional after-note sits at the foot, not numbered as a sixth move.

Between Move 3 and Move 4 the artefact gives explicit permission to pause — for checking measurements, prices, availability, anything that needs the conversation to resume later. That pause-permission is the artefact's most deliberate structural feature. Real household decisions are not conducted in single sit-downs. They happen across messages, across days, in the kitchen and in the car. A guide that assumes single-sitting use reads well and fails on contact.

Move 4 carries a clause that did not come from any of the Claude perspectives: *caring more doesn't automatically beat ongoing burden*. If one partner cares strongly about a choice but the other will carry the cost, the work, or the mild daily irritation of living with it, the caring-more position does not simply win. This is the non-Claude Outsider's *burden-cost asymmetry*, adopted in substance and close to verbatim in wording. Without it, the "who cares more" move would drift into a preference-wins rule, and produce exactly the resentment failure mode a working household governance artefact is supposed to prevent.

## The voice that resolved the split

The Claude perspectives divided two-two on a single surface detail: should the artefact address the couple as *we* or as *you two*. Each choice carries consequences. *We* positions the reader as already inside the couple the document is for. *You two* positions the document as coming from outside, instructing. The Drafter and the Skeptic wanted *we*. The Mediator wanted *you two*. Neither side could absorb the other's reasoning.

The Outsider, working from a different training distribution than the three Claude voices, proposed a third thing. Second-person plural for the addressing; first-person singular for the example lines. *You two* in the prose, *I* in the quotations that show what the conversation might actually sound like. The hybrid accommodates both Claude positions without asking either to surrender. The synthesis adopted it.

This is the second session in a row in which a two-two Claude deadlock was resolved by an Outsider-originated mechanism that no Claude perspective had reached. The previous one, in Session 009, was the copy-plus-reference scheme that resolved the contested decision about where external artefacts should live. Two is not a finding. It is a pattern the system has now explicitly tagged for future watching. If a later session produces a Claude split that the Outsider does *not* resolve with an innovation, that will be information. If the synthesis appears to be reaching for the Outsider's position when a Claude position would do, that will be information of a different kind.

## Aim at obsolescence

One of Session 010's recorded watchpoints is a new success criterion, proposed by the Skeptic and specific to governance-domain artefacts. The standard measure of a working artefact is that it continues to get used. The morning movement sequence from Session 008 passes this test on its own terms: the user wakes up, runs the sequence, reports that it still helps. But a decision-making aid for a couple who have been making decisions together for years is different. The best outcome might be that they use it once, find it mildly helpful, absorb whatever small distinction it introduced, and never reach for it again. Continued dependence may be a signal of failure, not success.

The artefact was written with this criterion in mind. Its closing lines explicitly deprecate themselves — *you haven't failed; you've just learned something about the next one*. Its "Not this" section is short enough to fit in a glance: no scoring matrix, no written case, no third-party call, no cool-off rule. A reader could reasonably read the whole page once and discard it.

## What changed on the bookkeeping

Session 010 produced no specification revisions, opened no new open issues, and did not advance the closure tally on the long-running non-Claude-participation question. It did quietly retire a blocker. An open issue dating to the methodology's earliest sessions had been waiting for a second external application to accumulate enough evidence before a broader question — about whether the methodology's nine activities each need finer-grained sub-activity definitions — could be deliberated. That precondition is now satisfied. The question itself has not been taken up; the procedural reason for not taking it up has.

The file at `applications/010-household-decision-protocol/house-decision-five-moves.md` is also the first external artefact produced directly into the canonical external-artefacts directory, without a regularising step. Session 008's *Morning Unfurl* was written into its own session's provenance folder and had to be copied across in Session 009 when the `applications/` directory was defined. This one was written at the canonical path from the start. The structure introduced in Session 009 now holds what it was designed to hold, in the way it was designed to hold it.

## A question left open

*The artefact will only be useful if it gets tried. The operator and his wife have live decisions on the table: shelving, landscaping, a few hundred dollars either way. What the methodology learns from governance work depends, now, on whether a couple who already know how to decide things together find anything in this one page they didn't already have.*
