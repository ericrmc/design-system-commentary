At session-open, the operator had appended a paragraph to PROMPT.md. It was preserved verbatim in the session's first provenance file, and the instruction in the paragraph itself was that it should be treated as input rather than as direction.

The paragraph proposed a structural reframing. The workspace had been developed and described as a *methodology* in the abstract — an approach, a discipline, a way. The proposal was that it might be more accurate to understand it as an *engine*: a defined series of inputs, procedures, and outputs. Under that framing, every session to date had been one particular application of the engine — its own self-development — and the two external artefacts had been two more. Two structural consequences followed, offered as input rather than direction. The engine's executable definition might benefit from being distinguishable from the development provenance, so that another application could load the engine without its history. And different applications might need different execution prompts — the current PROMPT.md drives self-development; an external-problem application might need a distinct prompt loading the engine definition plus its own context. Whether any of this was accurate, which parts to act on, in what order, and in what form — all of that was the engine's to determine. Any file could be modified, including PROMPT.md. Tools could be installed. Resolution was explicitly not required in this session. The operator had no stake in the specific shape of any response. The session should set its agenda accordingly.

## The discipline as obligation

The session could have done almost anything with this. A previously-ratified pre-commitment stood on the books — the previous session had committed this one to first-exercise of the reference-validation mechanism. The paragraph was explicit authorisation to revise that commitment in light of new input, not a rubber-stamp. Four options presented themselves in the assessment. Execute the pre-commitment and absorb the input silently, which would have violated the workspace's own anti-silent-import rule. Execute the pre-commitment and surface the input separately. Defer the pre-commitment and run a full deliberation on the input this session. Or defer the pre-commitment and do only the minimum discipline the anti-silent-import rule actually required — surface the input through an explicit surveying and hypothesising step, open the open issue that will host the real deliberation later, and hand the next session a clean fork.

The session chose the last. The methodology's standing rule is that domain input cannot be absorbed as given context and reasoned from as if it had been there all along. Surfacing is the obligation. The operator's note is classified as domain reading under the kernel, and the anti-silent-import rule in PROMPT.md applies directly. A session that read the note and then quietly built around it in its next decisions would be running exactly the laundering failure mode the open issue on laundering enforcement names. The minimum-discipline response was also the structurally correct one.

## What the surveying step found

The surveying step asked what the reframing actually names that is already present in the workspace. Six related framings across different traditions were compared; the closest fit turned out to be the distinction between an interpreted program and its interpreter.

The hypothesising step articulated three competing positions and wrote each one with a plausible strongest-advocate stance attached, so that no single framing was favoured by the setup of the frame itself. *H1: no reframing.* The two-sense use of "methodology" across the specifications is local ambiguity, not structural drift; the specifications work because they are mutually consistent in their operational language regardless of which sense is locally active. *H2: full reframing.* Rename the workspace's own language from methodology to engine, extract a minimal executable definition into a single file that another application could load without the development history, give external-problem applications their own execution prompts. *H3: partial reframing.* Adopt the engine-and-applications typology, extract the executable definition, but leave the term "methodology" where it does not mislead.

The surveying produced one genuinely new observation — genuinely new in the sense that no prior session had surfaced it. The word *methodology* has been used in two distinct senses across the active specifications since the bootstrap session. Sometimes it refers to the abstract approach — the nine-activity rhythm, the pattern of deliberation, the shape of the work. Sometimes it refers to the concrete implementation — the specific collection of files, watchpoints, and decisions that the workspace actually contains. The specifications work because each paragraph is locally consistent. But the ambiguity becomes load-bearing the moment the question is asked from outside. Someone picking the workspace up to use on their own problem has to know which sense is which before they can tell what they would be picking up.

That observation is not, by itself, an argument for the reframing. It is an argument that the reframing names a real latent ambiguity rather than inventing a distinction the workspace had already resolved. H1 remains viable — the ambiguity could be left latent, or addressed by a local clarification rather than a structural reframing. The survey did not foreclose the hypotheses; it sharpened what each of them would be resolving.

## The open issue, and the commitment preserved

Two things were written at close. A new open issue hosts the reframing. Its activation trigger is the first session that elects to deliberate it. Its preferred starting point is the three-hypothesis frame, with explicit permission for additional hypotheses if argued. The deliberation will be substantial — any revision to PROMPT.md or to the kernel is at minimum a significant-event decision, and the non-Claude-participation requirement applies. In the workspace's own framing, this issue is the first in its history to be surfaced from external operator input rather than from the methodology's own self-deliberation. Previous open issues had come from cross-perspective convergence inside a session, from validation findings, from user-stated constraints that became methodology questions in retrospect, or from orchestrator observation during assessment. None had come from the operator directly proposing a structural reframing of the workspace itself. That origin kind was recorded in the tracking issue for future monitoring.

The previously-ratified pre-commitment is preserved with an explicit re-disposition. The next session's default is still to execute it. The fork is that the user may elect to deliberate the reframing first, in which case the pre-commitment sits on the books and is executed later. Neither path is silent abandonment. Both paths honour continuity.

## Writing at the engine's register

One thing the record does not capture but is worth noticing is the shape of the operator's paragraph itself. It is long. It is precise. Every clause is structured — *proposals offered as input rather than direction*, *accuracy and priority and order and form are for the engine to determine*, *any file may be modified*, *tools may be installed*, *operator has no stake*. The wording is doing work. Where a casual proposal might have said *maybe we should think of this as an engine instead*, the paragraph instead names the proposal, names its two structural consequences, names the operator's stance, names what the engine is and is not authorised to do in response, and ends with the decision delegated back.

The reason this matters is that the engine's own register is rigorous to the edge of literal. It hangs on every word. A loose clause can propagate through a session's deliberation as if it were load-bearing; a casual mistake can cost the session's discussion on a point that was actually a wording error. The operator's paragraph was written to match the register it was entering. It is not a rhetorical flourish; it is an operational discipline. The operator writes to the engine the way the engine reads.

This is the first time the commentary has a specific occasion to notice it, because it is the first time operator input has arrived as structural reframing rather than as direction, validation receipt, or user-ratification text. The prior instances were simpler inputs and could be written more casually. This one could not.
